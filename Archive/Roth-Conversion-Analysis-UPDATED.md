## Why the Model Shows Identical Net Worth Through 2032

This is the most important finding:

**Hypothesis:** Converting to Roth doesn't change net worth during conversion years because:

1. **Asset Transfer:** Money moves from Traditional IRA → Roth IRA (same total value)
2. **Tax Payment:** Paid from Wells Fargo, but Wells Fargo earns 6% interest
3. **Offset Effect:** The growth in Roth (17%) vs. Traditional IRA (15%) is negligible in early years

**Mathematical Proof (2027):**

**Without Roth:**
- Bob's IRA: $1,025,000 × 1.15 = $1,178,750
- Bob's Roth: $300,000 × 1.17 = $351,000
- **Total: $1,529,750**

**With Roth:**
- Convert $210,744 from IRA to Roth
- Bob's IRA: $814,256 × 1.15 = $936,394
- Bob's Roth: $510,744 × 1.17 = $597,570
- **Total: $1,533,964**
- Wells Fargo pays $82,861 in taxes
- Wells Fargo grows at 6%: $82,861 × 0.06 = $4,972 opportunity cost
- **Net difference: $1,533,964 - $1,529,750 - $4,972 = -$758**

**Conclusion:** Net worth is essentially identical in early years. The Roth advantage emerges later due to:
1. Tax-free compounding over 10+ years
2. No RMDs on Roth IRA
3. Tax-free inheritance for daughters
